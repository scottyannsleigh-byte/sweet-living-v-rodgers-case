## 2. THE LAW (each verified today)

1. **Art. I, § 22, Florida Constitution:** *"The right of trial by jury shall be secure
   to all and remain inviolate."*
2. **Fla. R. Civ. P. 1.430(a):** the right of trial by jury as declared by the
   Constitution or by statute *"shall be preserved to the parties inviolate."*
3. **Fla. R. Civ. P. 1.430(d) — the decisive sentence:** *"A demand for trial by jury
   may not be withdrawn without the consent of the parties."* Once demanded, not even
   the **demanding party alone** can un-demand it — it takes **both sides' consent**.
4. **§ 51.011(3), Fla. Stat.** (summary procedure, which governs evictions): a party
   may demand a jury **"in any pleading"** or by separate paper served **not later than
   5 days after the action comes to issue.** Her demand was **in a pleading** (DIN 24).
5. **"At issue" timing:** under Fla. R. Civ. P. 1.440(a), an action is at issue only
   **after motions directed to the last pleading are disposed of.** Her motions (to
   dismiss, to quash, to determine rent) were **never disposed of** — so the action
   arguably **never came to issue**, and the § 51.011(3) window **never closed**. Her
   demand was timely on the rule's own arithmetic. In the alternative, DIN 29 asked for
   leave to demand out of time — a request the court had discretion to grant and
   **never ruled on**.
6. **Denial of a constitutionally guaranteed, properly demanded jury trial is treated
   as structural — reversible per se, not subject to harmless-error rescue.**
7. **The "authorized by law" hook for § 51.011(3):** § 83.60(1), Fla. Stat., expressly
   contemplates jury trial in eviction actions — *"the court or the jury, as the case
   may be, shall determine the amount, if any, by which the rent is to be reduced"* —
   and § 723.061(3) routes Ch. 723 evictions through the same summary procedure.
8. **The constitutional line (second-pass research — VERIFY each at the law library
   before citing):** Art. I, § 22 preserves the jury right as it existed in **1845**;
   eviction is the modern analogue of **ejectment**, an action at law tried to juries
   at common law. Supporting authorities identified: *Pernell v. Southall Realty*, 416
   U.S. 363 (1974) (U.S. Supreme Court: jury right in eviction/possession actions);
   *Baldwin Sod Farms, Inc. v. Corrigan*, 746 So. 2d 1198 (Fla. 4th DCA 1999) (read at
   1202); *In re Estate of Howard*, 542 So. 2d 395 (Fla. 1st DCA 1989) (1845 test);
   *Mastriana v. Brown Bros. Harriman*, 410 So. 3d 1223 (Fla. 4th DCA 2025) (most
   recent statement of the test). No 2d DCA case directly on point was found — be
   candid in the brief that this may be a question of first impression there.
   (Earlier drafts referenced *K.D. Lewis Enterprises Corp. v. Smith* — do NOT cite
   it; second-pass verification found it does not hold what was claimed.)

## 3. THE ARGUMENT — a chain with no weak link

1. A jury was **demanded in a pleading** (DIN 24) and by **motion** (DIN 29), invoking
   the constitutional right. The clerk **docketed the case as a jury case.**
2. Under **Rule 1.430(d), the demand could not be withdrawn without BOTH parties'
   consent.** There was no withdrawal, no consent, and no waiver colloquy. The
   judgment's *"abandoned"* line (DIN 44 ¶ C) is a **legal impossibility as applied to
   a jury demand** — a recess accepted at a hearing is not "consent of the parties,"
   and abandonment-by-silence is precisely what 1.430(d) forbids.
3. The court then **resolved contested fact questions from the bench** — whether a
   tenancy existed, what the negotiations were, the effect of the title — the exact
   questions the jury was demanded for.
4. The court **never ruled on DIN 29 at all** — not granted, not denied, not addressed.
   Entering final judgment with a pending, unruled jury-trial motion compounds the
   error and defeats any suggestion of implied waiver.
5. Denial of the demanded jury is **per se reversible**; it does not require showing the
   outcome would have differed.

## 4. THEIR BEST COUNTERARGUMENT — and why it fails here

⚠️ **BRIEFING INSTRUCTION: the jury issue and the no-tenancy issue are ONE argument —
brief them together.** The chain: no prospectus was delivered and tenancy was denied in
writing (§ 723.011; the 12/31/2025 letter) → the registry-deposit duty in
§ 83.60(2)/§ 723.063(2) binds a *tenant/mobile home owner* raising defenses, which she
was not → the "deposit default" was therefore not a lawful statutory default → the jury
demand survived and Rule 1.430(d) barred its "abandonment" → the bench judgment denied
the constitutional right. If the no-tenancy premise is briefed separately and fails,
the other side will recast the default as a lawful § 83.60(2) procedural default that
extinguished the jury right. Interlock them.

**Precision correction (from the verified June 3 judgment, DIN 41 ¶ B):** the motion
for the tenancy hearing (DIN 26) was **"legally deficient and therefore DENIED"** — it
was ruled on, not ignored. The argument is that the DENIAL was error because *Frey*
and *Grimm* required the evidentiary hearing before any deposit obligation or default
could attach — not that the court failed to rule. (The jury motions, DIN 24/29,
remained unruled; DIN 44 ¶ C then declared everything "abandoned.")

**They will also say:** the judicial default extinguished the jury right — a defaulted
party admits liability, and juries survive default only for unliquidated damages (of
which an eviction has none).

**The rebuttal (brief these together, not separately):**
- The "default" is itself the **lead error on appeal**: it was entered against a party
  who had **three answers on file**, a **pending personal-jurisdiction motion**, and a
  **pending jury demand**, who **appeared in person** at the hearing — and it was
  entered *without* the evidentiary hearing that *Frey* and *Grimm* make mandatory when
  the existence of the tenancy is the disputed question. A court cannot **bootstrap**:
  it cannot use an improper default to erase the constitutional demand that should have
  routed those very fact disputes to a jury.
- Leaving a courtroom after declining a five-minute recess is not a *failure to
  defend* — the answers and motions remained on file; a "judicial default" against an
  appearing, answering defendant is a sanction, and striking pleadings as a sanction
  has its own strict requirements (express findings of willfulness) that the judgment
  nowhere makes.
- Sequence matters: the jury demand (March–May) **predates** the June 18 default. The
  right attached first.
