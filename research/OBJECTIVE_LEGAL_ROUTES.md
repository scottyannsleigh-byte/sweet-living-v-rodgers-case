## ROUTE 1 — Verified Motion to Disqualify: Fla. R. Gen. Prac. & Jud. Admin. 2.330

**Verified against the current rule text. Subdivision letters below are from the current
rule (they were re‑lettered by amendment — older internet templates use different
letters; follow these).**

This is the mechanism you asked for — "something the judge himself can't deny" — because
the rule contains **three lock‑ins**:

1. **Rule 2.330(h) — he is forbidden from disputing your facts.** On an initial motion,
   the judge *"may determine only the legal sufficiency of the motion and shall not pass
   on the truth of the facts alleged."* He must take your facts as true; the only
   question is whether those facts would put a reasonable person in fear of an unfair
   hearing.
2. **The *Bundy* trap.** *Bundy v. Rudd*, 366 So. 2d 440 (Fla. 1978): a judge who goes
   beyond legal sufficiency and *"attempt[s] to refute the charges of partiality"* has
   *"exceeded the proper scope of his inquiry and on that basis alone established grounds
   for his disqualification."* If he argues with the motion, he disqualifies himself.
3. **Rule 2.330(l) — the 30‑day dead‑man switch.** The judge must rule *"immediately,
   but no later than 30 days after the service"* of the motion. **If he does not rule
   within 30 days, the motion is deemed granted** and the moving party may seek
   reassignment. Silence = granted.

If he *denies* a legally sufficient motion, the remedy is a **petition for writ of
prohibition** — review by a *different* court. (Since the January 1, 2021 jurisdiction
change (ch. 2020‑61, amending § 26.012, Fla. Stat.), appeals from county court final
judgments lie in the **district courts of appeal**, so the prohibition petition
concerning this county judge belongs in the **Second DCA** — the same court already
handling your appeal. Confirm the filing mechanics with Self‑Help.)

### 1a. What the motion must contain — Rule 2.330(c), checked item by item

A motion that misses one of these gets denied as "legally insufficient" no matter how
good the facts are. The current rule requires the motion to:

1. **Be in writing** (an oral motion made during a hearing must be *"promptly reduced to
   writing"* — subd. (g));
2. **"Allege specifically the facts and reasons"** relied on, **and identify the date the
   facts were discovered** — put the discovery date in the motion explicitly;
3. **Be sworn to or affirmed by you** — by signing the motion under oath or attaching a
   separate affidavit. The no‑notary option: Fla. Stat. **§ 92.525(2)** lets you verify
   with this exact sentence typed **immediately above your signature**:
   > *"Under penalties of perjury, I declare that I have read the foregoing motion and
   > that the facts stated in it are true."*
4. **State the dates of any previously granted disqualification motions** in the case
   (for you: none — say so);
5. Include a **good‑faith certification**. The rule words this as a certification *"by
   the attorney for the party"*; you have no attorney, so sign a certification yourself
   that the motion and your statements are made in good faith, and **ask Self‑Help how
   the county court wants this handled for self‑represented parties.**

**Ground (subd. (e)):** that you *"reasonably fear[] that [you] will not receive a fair
trial or hearing because of specifically described prejudice or bias of the judge."*
The fear is judged objectively — would a reasonable person in your shoes fear it.

### 1c. Timing — urgent

**Rule 2.330(g):** file *"within a reasonable time not to exceed 20 days after
discovery"* of the facts. If the "Desmond needs his money" statement was made at the

pages. **Call Self‑Help first thing tomorrow.**

### 1d. What disqualification gets you — Rule 2.330(j)

Once a judge is disqualified, *"[p]rior factual or legal rulings by a disqualified judge
may be reconsidered and vacated or amended by a successor judge"* — on a motion for
reconsideration filed **within 30 days of the disqualification order**. That is the
lawful undo button for the tainted rulings.

**Caveats to raise with Self‑Help (be straight about these):**
- Reconsideration by the successor judge is **discretionary** ("may"), not automatic.
- Your case is **on appeal**, so the county court's hands are partly tied. The trial
  court still controls what remains below — the **writ of possession**, any
  **attorney's‑fee motion**, and **everything on remand if you win the appeal**. If the
  appeal succeeds, you want the case returning to a **different judge**. Ask: *"Can the
  successor judge reconsider the final judgment under 2.330(j) while the appeal is
  pending, or does the appellate court have to relinquish jurisdiction first?"*

## ROUTE 2 — The appeal: errors visible on the face of the record

No credibility contests here — these come from the plaintiff's own filings and the
judge's own signed judgment. (First protect the appeal itself: show‑cause response due
**≈ July 11**, $300 fee or § 57.081 indigency due **≈ Aug 5** — see the master report.)

### 2a. Default for non‑deposit of rent without the evidentiary hearing the law requires

**What the judgment says (DIN 44):** ¶ 1 finds *"Defendant held valid title to the
mobile home"*; ¶ 4 defaults you because you *"failed to deposit the past due and ongoing
rent into the registry of the court as required by law."*

**What the law says:** the registry‑deposit machinery — § 83.60(2) for ordinary
tenancies (*"absolute waiver of the tenant's defenses other than payment… immediate
default judgment… writ of possession to issue without further notice or hearing"*) and
§ 723.063(2)'s parallel rule for mobile‑home lot tenancies — **presupposes a tenancy.
When the occupant claims a right to the property other than as a tenant, the court must
hold an evidentiary hearing on the existence of the tenancy BEFORE it may order
deposits or default for non‑deposit:**

- ***Frey v. Livecchi*, 852 So. 2d 896 (Fla. 4th DCA 2003)** — occupants claimed under a
  purchase contract; trial court ordered registry deposits and entered a default
  eviction judgment **without an evidentiary hearing**; the Fourth DCA **reversed the
  default final judgment**, holding the court was *"required to conduct an evidentiary
  hearing"* first.
- ***Grimm v. Huckabee*, 891 So. 2d 608 (Fla. 1st DCA 2005)** — same: where occupancy is
  claimed under a purchase‑and‑sale right rather than a tenancy, an evidentiary hearing
  is required to determine the basis of the right of occupancy before eviction; Chapter
  83's summary procedure does not simply apply.

**Your fit:** you claimed *ownership* (an $18,000 purchase), denied any tenancy ever
existed (the park itself **denied your tenancy in writing on 12/31/2025**), and filed a
**Motion to Determine Rent and Request for Evidentiary Hearing on the Existence of a
Tenancy (DIN 26)** citing exactly these cases. No such hearing was held. The judgment
then found you the **owner** in ¶ 1 and defaulted you as a **tenant** in ¶ 4. The
judgment contradicts itself on its face.

### 2b. The plaintiff used the wrong statutory machinery — and skipped the ground that fit

All verified against the statute text:

- **Chapter 723 governs.** The complaint itself alleges a park with **10+ lots** and
  that **you own the mobile home** — a Chapter 723 lot tenancy, and the complaint cites
  **§ 723.061(1)(c)(1)**. Chapter 723 supplies its own grounds (§ 723.061(1)(a)–(e)),
  its own notice rules (§ 723.061(4): written notice, **posted on the premises AND sent
  by certified or registered mail**, deemed delivered 5 days after postmark), and its
  own defense/deposit scheme (§ 723.063).
- **Yet plaintiff ran the Chapter 83 machinery:** a Form 1.923(a) **residential eviction
  summons** (5‑business‑day answer, § 83.60(2) registry regime) — the same machinery the
  default was later built on.
- **The mismatch inside § 723.061 itself:** for **nonpayment**, § 723.061(1)(a) requires
  the default to continue **5 days after delivery of written demand** for *lot rental*
  owed by a *mobile home owner tenant*; for a **rule violation**, § 723.061(1)(c) has a
  7‑day cure/vacate notice structure. And — this is the one they avoided —
  **§ 723.061(1)(e)** is the *specific, tailor‑made ground* for this exact situation:
  *"Failure of the purchaser… of a mobile home situated in the mobile home park to be
  qualified as, and to obtain approval to become, a tenant"* — with its own 7‑day
  notice‑to‑vacate procedure. **Sweet Living declared you an unapproved purchaser on
  December 31, 2025 — and then sued you for nonpayment of rent as a tenant.** Their own
  denial letter is the admission that the theory they sued under didn't fit.
- **§ 723.059 (verified quote):** a purchaser *"may become a tenant… subject to the
  approval of the park owner, but such approval may not be unreasonably withheld,"* and
  the purchaser *"may cancel or rescind the contract for purchase… if the purchaser's
  tenancy has not been approved by the park owner 5 days before the closing."* The
  statute contemplates the sale being **unwound** when approval fails — not the park
  keeping the home, the lot, and suing the denied purchaser for rent.

## ROUTE 3 — Fla. R. Civ. P. 1.540(b)(4): a void judgment can be attacked at ANY time

If service of process was defective, the judgment is **void**, and a motion under
1.540(b)(4) has **no filing deadline** — it does not depend on the rehearing window that
already closed. Record facts supporting it:
- Return of service filed **~3 months** into the case (DIN 23);
- Papers directed to **3929 Argyle St.** — a home with **no mailbox** that you were
  **locked out of and never occupied** — while plaintiff's own 5‑Day Notice shows they
  knew your residence was **13721 94th Ave., Seminole**;
- The process server's status log inside DIN 23 records an **attempt** at Seminole
  (5/11/2026, "no answer… taped business card") — an attempt is not completed service.
A denial of a solid 1.540(b)(4) motion is itself appealable — and if disqualification
has been granted by then, a **successor judge** hears it.

## WHAT *NOT* TO DO (your credibility is a legal asset — protect it)

- **Never** put "bribed," "paid off," "friends," or "corrupt" in any filing. Rule
  2.330(c) requires a good‑faith certification and § 92.525 puts you under penalty of
  perjury; unprovable accusations risk sanctions and give them a reason to ignore the
  provable record.
- **Don't** let the disqualification motion crowd out the DCA deadlines — only the
  appeal can erase the judgment outright.
- **Don't** mention the JQC/Bar/DBPR complaints in court filings, and don't file them as
  leverage — they are separate tracks.
- **Don't** overstate. Example of the discipline that wins: Chapter 723 has its *own*
  registry‑deposit rule (§ 723.063(2), also with "absolute waiver / immediate default"
  language) — so the argument is **not** "no deposit could ever be required"; it is
  "**no deposit default may be entered before the court holds the evidentiary hearing
  on whether a tenancy exists at all**" (*Frey*; *Grimm*), which the court never held.
- **Keep every new fact sworn and dated.** Everything in this memo works because it is
  already in a signed, filed document.
