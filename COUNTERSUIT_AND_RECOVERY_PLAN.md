## PART 1 — POSTURE: "counter-suit" vs. new lawsuit

The eviction case is closed and on appeal, so you cannot simply file a counterclaim in
it now. Your money claims go in a **separate civil action** (Pasco County Court handles
claims to $50,000; small claims to $8,000; your indigency status under § 57.081 covers
trial-court filing fees too — same application).

**⚠️ One question a lawyer must answer before filing against Sweet Living:** Florida's
compulsory-counterclaim rule (Fla. R. Civ. P. 1.170(a)) can bar claims arising from the
same transaction that were not raised in the first suit. Your defenses were cut off by
a default entered over pending motions — which itself is being appealed — and several
claims (and the claims against **Sally Potes**, a non-party) fall outside the rule. But
sequencing matters: **winning the appeal first cleans everything**, and one claim
(malicious prosecution) *requires* winning it first. Ask Self-Help/counsel: *"Given the
default and pending appeal, which of my affirmative claims should wait for the mandate,
and which can be filed now?"*

## PART 3 — COLLECTABILITY (why a judgment here is worth more than paper)

The usual heartbreak in small-plaintiff cases is winning and collecting nothing. Not here:

- **Desmond Surujbali is the DBPR-registered owner, personally, of ten mobile home
  parks (710 lots) in Hillsborough, Polk, and Manatee counties** — real property in his
  own name.
- A Florida money judgment becomes a **lien on real property** when a certified copy is
  recorded in a county's official records (§ 55.10). Record in **all three counties**
  and the judgment attaches to everything he owns there — it collects itself when he
  sells or refinances, and it accrues statutory interest.
- **Reaching him personally:** corporate officers are personally liable for torts they
  **personally commit or direct**. He signed the 7-day notice containing the "currently
  residing" statement, signed the sworn non-military statements, appeared personally,
  and is the corporation's only officer. Sue **Sweet Living, Inc. AND Desmond Surujbali
  AND Sally Potes** and let discovery sort the agency lines.
- Potes alone may be judgment-proof — which is exactly why the agency/joint-venture
  facts (your affidavit: she "works together with the investor," coordinated with the
  on-site manager, used their portal) matter: they connect the $18,000 to defendants
  who own 710 lots.

## PART 6 — WHAT NOT TO DO (each of these can wreck the claims above)

- **Never threaten criminal prosecution to collect money.** The § 772.11 demand is a
  statutory form — it demands payment, period. Writing "pay or I'll call the State
  Attorney" can itself be a crime (extortion) and kills settlements. Report the title
  fraud to FLHSMV/SAO **separately** (`filings/08`) and never link the two in writing.
- **Don't file the civil-theft COUNT pro se.** Send the demand letter (safe, required);
  let a lawyer decide about the count (fee-shifting cuts both ways).
- **Don't sell or move the home without documenting its condition first** — photos,
  video, date-stamped, before anything else.
- **Don't discuss settlement numbers on the phone** — writing only, so the record stays
  clean for the FCCPA/FDUTPA claims.
- **Don't let this replace the appeal.** Every claim on this page is stronger the day
  the appeal is won; several exist only if it is.
