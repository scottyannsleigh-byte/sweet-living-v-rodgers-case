### What this does and does not prove — read both halves

**The careful half.** Fla. Stat. § 723.003(13) defines "park owner" as "an owner **or
operator** of a mobile home park." So Sweet Living could lawfully act as the park owner
if it is the *operator* under some agreement with Chang, or if it bought the park
recently and DBPR's records lag. **This record alone does not destroy their standing.**

**The sharp half.** The complaint against you doesn't say "operator." It alleges
Plaintiff **owns** the mobile home park. The State of Florida's own registry says
someone else owns it. That gives you:
- **A concrete factual contradiction of a sworn pleading allegation**, supporting the

## 2. THE § 723.011 CHAIN — why "no enforceable rental agreement" is the cleanest kill

The statute, quoted exactly (verified today):

- § 723.011(1): *"In a mobile home park containing 26 or more lots, the park owner
  shall file a prospectus with the division."* — Zephyr has **82**.
- § 723.011: *"**Prior to entering into an enforceable rental agreement** for a mobile
  home lot, the park owner **shall deliver to the homeowner a prospectus approved by
  the division**."*
- The rules and regulations *"shall be deemed to be incorporated into the rental
  agreement"* — i.e., the rules bind a person **through** a rental agreement formed
  after prospectus delivery.

Now put their own documents against it:

| Their claim | The statutory problem |
|---|---|
| Complaint: you had an **"oral agreement"** to pay $558/mo lot rent | An enforceable lot rental agreement requires **prior delivery of an approved prospectus** — which they never made (and could not have made: they **denied your tenancy in writing on 12/31/2025**) |
| 7‑Day Notice: you violated **"Paragraph 2 of the Zephyr Mobile Home Park Rules and Regulations"** | Rules bind through the rental agreement/prospectus. No delivered prospectus + no approved tenancy = no contractual hook. And § 723.061(1)(c) requires a **"properly promulgated"** rule — the prospectus was approved in **1985**; whether today's "Paragraph 2" was ever filed and approved as an amendment is checkable at DBPR **[VERIFY — same phone call as above]** |
| The whole case: a **tenant nonpayment eviction** | § 723.003(11) defines a mobile home owner/tenant as one who owns a home **"and rents or leases a lot."** You never rented or leased a lot — by their own denial letter. You fall outside both Chapter 723 tenancy and Chapter 83 tenancy — which is why the **evidentiary hearing on the existence of a tenancy** (your DIN 26; *Frey*; *Grimm*) was mandatory, and why the vehicle they chose was wrong from the first page |

**How to use it:** this is an appellate argument (it rides on the face of the complaint,
the denial letter, and the statute), a § 1.540(b)(4) support, and a DBPR complaint
enhancement. It does **not** need any witness to be believed — that's what makes it
strong.

**Honest limit:** don't argue "therefore I can live there free." The argument is
narrower and better: *the relationship they pleaded cannot legally exist, so the
summary eviction machinery (registry deposits, defaults) never applied to me — and the
money I paid for the home is governed by § 723.059's rescission scheme, not by rent.*

## 3. THE JUDGMENT'S OWN CITATION WORKS FOR YOU

The June 18 Final Judgment (DIN 44 ¶ 1) cites ***Green Tree Acceptance, Inc. v.
Zimerman*, 611 So. 2d 608 (Fla. 2d DCA 1993)** for the finding that you "held valid
title." Verified today: that case holds a purchaser's **failure to obtain the title
certificate does not prevent the passage of title from seller to buyer.**

Follow the logic the judgment itself sets up:
1. Per its own cited authority, **title passed to you on December 6, 2025** (the sale),
   regardless of the DMV paperwork the seller falsified.
2. So the court's first finding makes you the **owner** of the home.
3. An owner claiming under a purchase — not a tenancy — is **exactly** the person
   *Frey v. Livecchi* and *Grimm v. Huckabee* say cannot be defaulted for non‑deposit
   of "rent" **without an evidentiary hearing on whether any tenancy exists.**
4. Paragraph 4 of the same judgment defaults you as a tenant for non‑deposit of rent —
   without that hearing.

**The judgment's ¶ 1 supplies the premise that makes its ¶ 4 reversible.** That
belongs at the front of your appeal brief.
